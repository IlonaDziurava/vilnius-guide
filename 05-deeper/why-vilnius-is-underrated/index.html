    Why Vilnius Is Europe's Most Underrated Capital

    There is a certain type of European city that travellers discover and immediately feel possessive about. They come back from the trip and tell their friends with quiet urgency: you need to go before everyone else finds out. Vilnius is that city — except that somehow, decades after the rest of Eastern Europe was absorbed into the tourist circuit, it still hasn't been found out.

    This is strange. Vilnius is a capital city with a UNESCO-listed Old Town, a functioning bohemian republic within its borders, a specialty coffee scene that produces European champions, and a history so layered and violent and improbable that it reads like fiction. It is, by almost any measure, a remarkable place. And yet the queues that snake around the block in Prague, the selfie sticks raised like antennae in Tallinn's Town Hall Square, the tour groups moving through Kraków in tight, branded clusters — none of that has arrived here yet.

    The question worth asking is: why not?

    The Invisibility Problem

    Part of the answer is geographic imagination. For most Western Europeans, the Baltic states occupy a vague mental territory somewhere between Scandinavia and Russia — cold, flat, distant, and not entirely distinct from one another. Lithuania, Latvia, and Estonia are often treated as a single interchangeable entity, which does a disservice to all three and particularly to Vilnius, which is the most architecturally and historically complex of the three capitals.

    Tallinn gets the tourists because its Old Town is compact and photogenic and sits conveniently on the Helsinki ferry route. Riga gets attention for its Art Nouveau. Vilnius — larger, messier, more contradictory — tends to be skipped, or left for a second trip that often never happens.

    There is also the matter of narrative. Cities that attract tourists tend to have a single, legible story: Paris is romance, Prague is fairy-tale medieval, Dubrovnik is the Adriatic. Vilnius resists this. Its story is not simple or picturesque. It is a city that was, within living memory, the centre of one of the most vibrant Jewish cultures in the world — and then wasn't. A city that spent fifty years inside the Soviet Union and emerged with its identity not just intact but sharpened. A city that declared independence on television in 1990 while Soviet tanks rolled through its streets, and won.

    That story is harder to put on a postcard. But it is far more interesting than most of what fills the postcards elsewhere.

    The Paradox of the Old Town

    Vilnius Old Town is the largest in the Baltic states and one of the largest in all of Northern Europe — 3.6 square kilometres of Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque architecture, declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1994. Walk through it on a Tuesday morning in October and you will have it almost entirely to yourself.

    This is the central paradox of Vilnius: the very things that should make it famous keep it quiet. The Old Town is too large to feel like a theme park. Its architecture is too varied to be summarised in a single photograph. Its streets are slightly uneven, its courtyards slightly hidden, its churches numerous enough that visitors stop counting after the first dozen.

    The city rewards attention in a way that resists the quick visit. You cannot do Vilnius properly in an afternoon the way you can absorb the essential image of somewhere more visually concentrated. It asks you to slow down, to turn off the main street, to push open the unmarked door in the wall that leads to a courtyard you weren't expecting. Some travellers find this frustrating. The ones who give it time tend to become the people who come back.

    Užupis and the Seriousness of Play

    Every city has a bohemian neighbourhood. What most cities do not have is a bohemian neighbourhood that responded to the collapse of the Soviet Union by declaring itself a sovereign nation.

    Užupis — the name means "beyond the river" — did exactly that on April 1st, 1997. The date was chosen deliberately. The republic has a president, a cabinet, a flag, a currency, and an army of approximately twelve people. Its constitution, engraved on mirrored plaques on Paupio Street in twenty-three languages, includes the right to be idle, the right to be happy, and the right not to be obliged to be happy.

    It would be easy to read Užupis as an elaborate joke, and in one sense it is. But the best jokes in the Lithuanian tradition — a tradition shaped by occupation, displacement, and the need to say difficult things indirectly — are never only jokes. Užupis was founded by artists who had lived through the Soviet period, who understood what it meant to have your culture suppressed and your identity managed by an external power, and who chose to respond with something playful and absurd and defiantly their own. The joke has a point.

    Walking through Užupis now — past the galleries and the river and the angel on the column and the cats sitting in the windows of the studios — it is possible to feel something that is harder to find in cities that have been more thoroughly processed for tourism: the sense that the place actually belongs to the people who live in it.

    What the Food Scene Tells You

    A city's restaurant culture is a reasonable proxy for its overall self-confidence, and by that measure Vilnius in 2024 is a city that knows exactly what it is.

    The traditional dishes — cepelinai, šaltibarščiai, dark rye bread, the smoked meats from the markets — are not performed for tourists. They are what people here actually eat, and the best versions of them are found not in restaurants designed to look traditional but in places like Snekutis in Užupis, a canteen that has no particular interest in being discovered, and Halės market, where the vendors have been selling the same smoked fish and forest mushrooms for decades and have no plans to change.

    Alongside this, Vilnius has developed a modern restaurant scene of genuine quality. Sweet Root, consistently ranked among the best restaurants in the Baltics, serves tasting menus that take Lithuanian ingredients — fermented dairy, foraged herbs, cold-smoked fish — and treat them with the seriousness usually reserved for French or Nordic cuisine. This is not fusion or novelty. It is a culture deciding that its own food is worth taking seriously, which is a different and more interesting thing.

    The coffee scene makes the same argument. Vilnius baristas compete at European level and win. The independent cafes on Pilies Street and in the side streets off it serve better espresso than you will find in most Western European capitals. This did not happen by accident. It happened because a generation of Lithuanians who grew up after independence decided to build something excellent, and had the confidence to do it without looking over their shoulder.

    The Weight of the 20th Century

    Any honest account of Vilnius has to reckon with what happened here in the 1940s.

    Before the Second World War, Vilnius was one of the most important centres of Jewish life and learning in the world. The city was known as the Jerusalem of Lithuania. Its Jewish population numbered in the tens of thousands and had shaped its culture, its commerce, and its intellectual life for centuries.

    Between 1941 and 1944, almost all of them were murdered. The Paneriai forest, a few kilometres outside the city, is where most of the killings took place. The memorial there is quiet and unostentatious and devastating.

    This history is not hidden in Vilnius. The Museum of Occupations and Struggles — housed in the former KGB headquarters on Gedimino Avenue, where prisoners were held and tortured in the basement — addresses both the Nazi and Soviet occupations with directness and without evasion. The building itself carries the weight of what happened inside it.

    A city that is willing to put this history at the centre of its public life rather than at the margins of it is doing something more honest than many cities manage. It is also, for a visitor willing to engage with it, one of the most significant things Vilnius has to offer — the chance to understand the 20th century not as abstract history but as something that happened to specific people in specific streets that you can still walk down.

    Go Now

    The window for experiencing Vilnius as it currently is — genuinely unhurried, genuinely local, genuinely itself — is not guaranteed to stay open indefinitely. The flights are cheap, the hotels are affordable, and the city is in the middle of a cultural moment that it has earned through a history more difficult than most.

    The people who go now and pay attention will, in ten years, be the ones telling their friends with quiet urgency: you need to go before everyone else finds out.

    Some of them will already be too late.

# History of Vilnius

Vilnius has been fought over, occupied, rebuilt, and reinvented more
times than almost any other European capital. Understanding its history
makes walking through it a completely different experience.

---

## Foundation (1323)

Vilnius was founded by Grand Duke Gediminas, who according to legend
dreamed of an iron wolf howling from a hill — interpreted as a sign
to build a great city there. He wrote letters to European merchants
and craftsmen inviting them to settle, making Vilnius an unusually
cosmopolitan city from its earliest days.

---

## The Grand Duchy of Lithuania (14th–18th century)

At its peak, the Grand Duchy of Lithuania was one of the largest
states in Europe, stretching from the Baltic to the Black Sea.
Vilnius was its capital and cultural centre. The city's extraordinary
architectural diversity — Gothic, Renaissance, Baroque all in one
neighbourhood — comes from this long period of growth and wealth.

The Union of Lublin (1569) merged Lithuania with Poland into the
Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, which shaped the city's Polish
cultural influences for centuries.

---

## Russian Empire (1795–1918)

After the partitions of Poland, Vilnius fell under Russian control.
The Russians called it Vilna. The city remained a major cultural
centre — particularly for Jewish life. By the late 19th century,
Vilnius was one of the most important centres of Jewish learning
in the world, known as the "Jerusalem of Lithuania."

---

## The 20th Century — Occupation and Loss

The 20th century brought catastrophe. Vilnius changed hands repeatedly:

- **1918** — Lithuania declares independence, claims Vilnius as capital
- **1920** — Poland seizes Vilnius; Lithuania loses its own capital
  for nearly 20 years
- **1939** — Soviet Union transfers Vilnius back to Lithuania under
  the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact
- **1940** — Soviet occupation of Lithuania begins
- **1941–1944** — German occupation; the Jewish community of Vilnius,
  numbering around 100,000 people, is almost entirely murdered
- **1944–1990** — Soviet rule; the city is renamed Vilnius and
  industrialised; significant demographic changes as Lithuanians
  move in and Poles and others leave

---

## Independence (1990–present)

On 11 March 1990, Lithuania became the first Soviet republic to
declare independence. Moscow responded with military force —
in January 1991, Soviet troops attacked the Vilnius TV Tower,
killing 14 civilians. Lithuanians formed a human chain to protect
the parliament. The world watched. The Soviets backed down.

In September 1991, independence was internationally recognised.
Lithuania joined the EU and NATO in 2004.

---

## Where to See It

- **Museum of Occupations and Struggles** — the former KGB
  headquarters, now a museum documenting Soviet and Nazi occupation
- **Paneriai Memorial** — the site where 70,000–100,000 people,
  mostly Jewish, were murdered during the German occupation
- **Gediminas Tower** — the symbol of the medieval Grand Duchy
- **Gate of Dawn** — survived every occupation, has never been closed
